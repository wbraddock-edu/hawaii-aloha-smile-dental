Kids Dental Care: A Parent's Complete Guide

By Aloha Smile Dental Team · April 15, 2024 · 6 min read

Establishing excellent dental habits in childhood creates the foundation for a lifetime of oral health. As parents, the choices we make about our children's dental care — from when to start, to how we respond to dental anxiety — have lasting consequences. Here's a comprehensive guide for Maui parents on caring for their children's teeth at every stage.

Before the First Tooth: Gum Care

Oral care begins before teeth erupt. After feedings, gently wipe your baby's gums with a clean, damp gauze pad or soft cloth. This removes bacteria and establishes the habit of oral cleaning. It also helps your baby become comfortable with having their mouth touched, which makes the transition to toothbrushing easier when teeth arrive.

First Tooth, First Visit: The Dental Home

The American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry recommends establishing a dental home by age one — no later than 6 months after the first tooth appears. Early visits accomplish several things: they identify any developmental concerns early, they establish your child's baseline oral health record, they allow the dentist to guide you on appropriate feeding practices, and most importantly, they introduce dental visits as a normal, positive part of life before any anxiety can develop.

Toothbrushing for Children

Once teeth appear, brush them twice daily. For children under 3, use a soft-bristled toothbrush and a rice grain-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste. For ages 3-6, use a pea-sized amount. Children typically don't have the dexterity to brush effectively on their own until around age 7-8 — parents should brush for young children and assist and supervise older children. Electric toothbrushes can improve effectiveness for children who resist manual brushing.

Preventing Cavities in Baby Teeth

Baby teeth matter — they hold space for permanent teeth, support speech development, and allow proper chewing. "Baby bottle tooth decay" (also called early childhood caries) occurs when babies fall asleep with bottles containing anything other than water. Avoid putting juice, formula, or milk in bottles at bedtime. Limit juice to 4 ounces per day for toddlers and preschoolers. First-tooth dental visits allow us to apply fluoride varnish — a highly effective cavity prevention treatment for young children.
