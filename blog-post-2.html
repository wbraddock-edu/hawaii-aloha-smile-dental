Sugar and Oral Health: Hawaiian Desserts to Watch

By Aloha Smile Dental Team · May 28, 2024 · 6 min read

Hawaii has a rich food culture with influences from Japanese, Filipino, Portuguese, Chinese, Korean, and Native Hawaiian traditions. Many beloved local treats and desserts, while delicious, present real risks to oral health due to their sugar content, acidity, or sticky texture. Understanding which foods pose the greatest dental risk helps you make informed choices about your family's treats without eliminating the joy of local food culture.

How Sugar Damages Teeth

The cavity-causing bacteria in your mouth (primarily Streptococcus mutans) feed on sugar, producing acid as a byproduct. This acid demineralizes tooth enamel — the hard outer layer of the tooth — creating the soft spots that become cavities. The key factor isn't just the amount of sugar consumed but the frequency and duration of sugar exposure. Sipping a sweetened drink slowly over an hour exposes teeth to ongoing acid attack, while eating the same amount of sugar in one meal followed by water and brushing is significantly less damaging.

Local Foods to Be Mindful Of

Shave ice, while primarily water, is often doused in sweet syrups and sometimes condensed milk. The frequency of consumption and the duration of sipping are the main concerns. Mochi, with its sticky glutinous rice texture, clings to teeth and provides sustained sugar exposure. Malasadas and Portuguese sweet bread have high sugar and refined carbohydrate content that feeds cavity-causing bacteria. Poi, interestingly, has a relatively low sugar content and may actually have protective oral health properties due to its slightly basic pH. Dried fruits like dried mango and pineapple are very sticky and high in concentrated sugar — often worse than fresh fruit.

Smarter Snacking Strategies

You don't need to eliminate beloved local treats — you need to be strategic about when and how you consume them. Eating sugary treats as part of a meal (rather than as standalone snacks between meals) reduces the number of acid attack episodes your teeth experience. Drinking water during and after sweet foods helps rinse sugar from tooth surfaces. Chewing xylitol gum after meals and sweets neutralizes acid and reduces cavity-causing bacteria.

Fresh Local Fruits: Friend or Foe?

Fresh pineapple, mango, papaya, and guava are healthy foods with real nutritional value, but they are also acidic and contain natural sugars. They are far superior to processed sweets from a health standpoint, but eating large amounts frequently throughout the day can contribute to enamel erosion. Enjoy Hawaii's amazing fresh fruit as part of meals, rinse with water afterward, and don't brush for 30 minutes after consuming acidic foods.
